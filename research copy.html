Rational models of memory

My primary research interests lie in the urge to understand, not only what we observe about human memory, but also the reasons behind those observations. For example, forgetting in human memory has been observed in scientific studies from as early as 1885, but it is only in 1990 that we understand the reason behind forgetting: there is no need to continue storing information that becomes less and less relevant in the current envirnoment. I apply similar rational approaches to my research to undertand, not only what the human beahvior is in a given task, but most importantly what the optimal behavior is given the computational problem imposed by the memory task. These efforts will serve as a way to translate assumptions about peopleâ€™s goals and the structure of the memory environment into predictions about human behavior that we can test in empirical studies.

Psychologists have studied the rich behavioral patterns in free recall task for decades, however, it remains unclear why certain types of recall organization might be favored in the first place. In a recent project [1], we provide a rational analysis of the free recall task, deriving the optimal policy for recalling items under the internal representations and processes described by a mechanistic model that has been shown to capture memory search behavior. We show that the optimal policy for free recall is to start from the beginning of the list and then sequentially recall forwards, providing a rational account of the primacy and forward asymmetry effects typically observed in free recall.

In two undergraduate projects where I played the mentoring role [2-3], we showed that the extent that human categorization relies more on prototype representations or examplar representations depends on how accessible those representations are in their memory given the environmental statistics, consistent with a rational account [2]. In another project, we provided a Bayesian model on hindsight bias, where memory of an original event is biased towards post-event information. Moreover, we showed that the amount of bias depends on plausibility of the post-event information. This model provides a rational account of hindsight bias by considering memory recall as a statistical problem, where the goal is to reconstruct the original estimate using new evidence [3].

Once we understand how people optimally encode and recall information under cognitive constraints and the environment, we can alleviate the cognitive constraints and alter the environment to further boost their memory performance. This line of work has close connection with mnemonic techniques and AI assistants, which is the focus of several of my ongoing projects [5-7]. Ask me more about them!

The influence of prior knowledge on learning and memory

Studies have shown that the way that new information is absorbed is constanyly shaped by our prior knowledge. To extend this, we examine how our prior knowledge can affect not only learning new information itself, but also the way it is generalized later [1]. In another study, we demonstrate that prior knowledge affects whether episodic associations are encoded in a holistic representation or separate directional representations [2]. In an ongoing project, we examine how prior knowledge influences the temporal dynamics and recall performance during memory search [3].

Neuroimaging methods

The human brain functions by receiving an input, processesing information, and producing an output. To be able to isolate the cognitive process of interest from this cascade of events, we develope a method that can parse neural time series data into a sequence of latent cognitive stages [1]. In this way, we can model the trial-to-trial variability of when a cognitive process takes place.

Pooling neural imaging data across subjects requires aligning recordings from different subjects. We investigate a method that bypasses source-localization in aligning MEG subjects [2]. It transforms individual subject data to a low-dimensional common representational space where inter-subject consistency is maximized. We evaluate the robustness of this approach over both a synthetic dataset and an MEG dataset.

[1] Anderson, J.R., Zhang, Q., Borst, J.P.,& Walsh, M.M. (2016). The Discovery of Processing Stages: Extension of Sternberg's Method. Psychological Review, 123(5), 481. [link] [author manuscript]

[2] Zhang, Q., Borst, J.P., Kass, R.E.,& Anderson, J.R. (2017). Inter-Subject Alignment of MEG Datasets in a Common Representational Space. Human Brain Mapping. [link] [author manuscript]

Human memory and information processing

Psychological research has long sought to characterize mental processes (i.e. information processing stages) that give rise to observable behavior. In the past, by combining neural imaging data and cognitive modeling approaches, we have identified the sequences of mental stages underlying associative recognition task, working memory task, and memory-based arithmetic task. In particular, we have demonstrated:

The effect of inserting an additional mental process. Imagine that you are solving a math multiplication problem: in scenario 1, the calculation is based on numerical values only (e.g. 3 x 5); scenario 2 is exactly the same calculation as scenario 1, but some numerical values are replaced with symbols (e.g. 3 x C, where C = 5). We identified in this EEG study that, the additional efforts you put in the scenario 2 is more than just replacing C with 5 in your mind [1]. There is interference with the existing mental process when inserting an additional mental process.

This result also gives additional implication on the methodology side. In the fMRI literature, one often identifies neural correlates of a cognitive process by contrasting brain activation when the process is present versus absent. We demonstrated a scenario when this approach does not hold, with the inserted mental process interacting with the existing ones.

A detailed mapping of working memory retrieval in space and in time. Subjects memorized a list of faces, and were asked immediately afterwards, if a presented face was in the previously memorized set. Combining the temporal and spatial resolution in EEG and ECoG data of the same task, we identified early neural components that are consistent with visual encoding, followed by familiarity signal in the temporal cortex; once the memorized face set is activated, frontal cortex guides the decision by theta coupling with medial temporal lobe [2].

The effect of similarity on recognition memory. Think of when you're on a bus: the more a stranger looks similar to a friend that you knows, the more likely you would recognize her/him as your friend. In this EEG study, we identified a complex pattern of how such similarity affects different cognitive processes. Namely, the more similar the stranger is to your friend, the easier it is to recall your friend (i.e. faster retrieval), but the more difficult it is to decide he/she is not your friend (i.e. slower decision) [3].

There is an additional implication of this result on the methodology side. In the psychology literature, overall response times are often used to infer the speed of individual cognitive processes. We demonstrated a scenario where one experimental factor (i.e. similarity) can speed up one cognitive process but slow down another, making it difficult to identify their changes by observing response times alone. The method we developed to parse the neural data into a sequence of latent stages is particularly useful in this scenario.

Transfer of learning

Human can complete a wide variety of tasks well in life, not because they have the opportunities to learn each of them one by one, but that human is capable of transferring old knowledge to new situations. In this project, I examined how students are able to extend their previous problem solving skills to new but similar problems, and found neural signature that account for individual differences in the task performance. Over fMRI data, subjects who perceive different problems similarly (i.e. who show consistent and stable brain patterns both within-subject and across-subjects) perform better. The level of consistency at the beginning of the experiment is also predictive of their later performance.

[1] Zhang, Q., Anderson, J.R., & Kass, R.E. (2015). Consistency in Brain Activation Predicts Success in Transfer. Proceedings of the 37th Annual Conference of the Cognitive Science Society. [pdf]

[2] Zhang, Q., Anderson, J.R., & Kass, R.E. (2014). Consistency in Extending Problem-solving Procedures Indicates Expertise. Master's thesis report. [pdf]

Brain computer interface

In my undergraduate project during the time at UCSD, I tried to improve brain-computer interface (BCI) systems by incorporating the effect of feedback. BCI requires accurate classification of brain signal before sending control to an external device. There is continuous feedback to the BCI user whether such control is successful. User's response to the feedback has been shown in general to be a source of noise that can add to the non-stationarity of the brain signal; however, in this work, we show that the user's brain response to the feedback can be used to improve the BCI performance.

[1] Mousavi, M., Koerner, A.S., Zhang, Q., Noh, E.,& de Sa, V.R. (2017). Improving Motor Imagery BCI with User Response to Feedback. Brain-Computer Interfaces, 4(1-2), 74-86.[link] [author manuscript]

[2] Koerner, A.S., Zhang, Q., & de Sa, V.R. (2013). The effect of real-time positive and negative feedback on motor imagery performance. Proceedings of the Fifth International Brain-Computer Interface Meeting: Defining the Future. [pdf]
